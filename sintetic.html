SYNTHETIC SUPERHEAVY ELEMENTS

Definition: Synthetic superheavy elements are elements with an atomic number higher than 103, artificially created in laboratories. They do not naturally exist on Earth and are synthesized through nuclear reactions in particle accelerators.

Characteristics:

  • They are extremely unstable and decay rapidly.
  • They belong to the **transactinide elements**, found at the bottom of the periodic table.
  • Their physical and chemical properties are difficult to study due to their short lifetimes.
  • Their synthesis helps scientists understand **nuclear physics** and **atomic stability**.

List of synthetic superheavy elements:

  • Darmstadtium (Ds) - 110
  • Roentgenium (Rg) - 111
  • Copernicium (Cn) - 112
  • Nihonium (Nh) - 113
  • Flerovium (Fl) - 114
  • Moscovium (Mc) - 115
  • Livermorium (Lv) - 116
  • Tennessine (Ts) - 117
  • Oganesson (Og) - 118

Discovery: Most of these elements were discovered in the second half of the 20th century through experiments conducted in laboratories such as **Dubna (Russia), GSI Darmstadt (Germany), Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory (USA), and RIKEN (Japan)**.

Uses:

  • Scientific research on nuclear stability.
  • Studies on atomic and chemical properties.
  • Potential future applications in advanced technologies.

Effects on health and the environment: Since these elements are highly radioactive and unstable, they pose minimal health risks as they are never produced in large quantities. However, their synthesis processes generate radiation that must be carefully managed.
